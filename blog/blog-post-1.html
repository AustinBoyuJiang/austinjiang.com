Victor was initially seeking out natural philosophy or science to study the workings of the world as he “always has been imbued with a fervent longing to penetrate the secrets of nature.” (38) However, as he sees the nature of the world as an ocean, scientists are just children “picking up shells beside the great and unexplored ocean” (38), which is never possible to know the truth and essence of the world. Victor considers that there aren’t too many differences in knowledge between peasants and scientists. Once Victor‘s cognition is astonished by the explanation of electricity and galvanism formed by a great natural philosophy researcher, he believes that “nothing could or would ever be known” (40) and thus gives up his study.

Victor's pursuit of knowledge almost reached the point of obsession. Victor “enters with the greatest diligence into the search of the philosopher’s stone and the elixir of life” (39) and focuses on the elixir of life, aiming to “banish disease from the human frame and render man invulnerable to any but a violent death.” (39) However, in the same chapter, it is indicated that scientists are just children “picking up shells beside the great and unexplored ocean” (38) and “nothing could or would ever be known” (40), suggesting that Victor disregards the natural limits and humble position of humanity in the face of the vast unknown and reflecting his excessive ambition and his desire to achieve what is impossible. Also, to research the elixir of life, his goal is not just about improving human knowledge or health but about fundamentally altering the human condition, challenging the nature of life and death. It demonstrates his desire for god-like power and control, which is overambitious.

Although Victor considers the books he read before useless, Professor Kempe tells him what he studied is completely worthless and “nonsense” (45). Professor Kempe says, “These fancies which you have so greedily imbibed are a thousand years old and as musty as they are ancient” (46). However, Victor’s interest in science is founded on those visions that were denied by Professor Kempe, which he called “fancies” (46) and “nonsense” (45), and thus he can’t restore Victor's interest in science. In contrast, Professor Waldman says that although the ancient scientists “promised impossibilities and performed nothing” (47), "they penetrated into the recesses of nature" (47) and "performed miracles" (47). He eliminates Victor’s prejudice against modern scientists by consenting that those scientists had indeed made a “solid advantage of mankind” (49), which is Victor’s goal. Rather than belittle Victor’s knowledge, he recognizes Victor’s ability and encourages Victor to apply it. Because of Professor Waldman, Victor rediscovered his passion for science and ambitions to “explore unknown powers, and unfold to the world the deepest mysteries of creation.” (48) Additionally, the text in the story also implies Victor’s attitude toward the two professors through physiognomy: Professor Kempe is described as a “little squat man with a gruff voice and a repulsive countenance” (46), while Professor Waldman is “mild and attractive” (48) or with “greatest affability and kindness” (48).

“Besides, I had a contempt for the uses of modern natural philosophy. It was very different when the masters of the science sought immortality and power; such views, although futile, were grand." (46) shows Victor Frankenstein's dismissal of modern scientific pursuits. He considers that only the pursuit of the elixir of life and powers, which is detachment from the practical and ethical considerations of science, is worthy of matching his aim and mission and aligning himself with this grandiose vision, highlighting his inflated sense of self and ambition..

“So much has been done, exclaimed the soul of Frankenstein—more, far more, will I achieve; treading in the steps already marked, I will pioneer a new way, explore unknown powers, and unfold to the world the deepest mysteries of creation," (48) indicates that Victor has the ambition to surpass previous scientific achievements. His proclamation of exploring "unknown powers" and unfolding "the deepest mysteries of creation," which is contrary to his previous claim that “nothing could or would ever be known” by human beings (40), demonstrates excessive ambition and a sense of superiority to achieve an unrealistic goal.

Although M. Krempe is “with a repulsive physiognomy and manners” (50), it is “not on that account the less valuable” (50) in his academic study. It indicates that rather than a person’s countenance and manners, Victor regards and respects one by his study value, showcasing Victor’s admiration and belief in science. Furthermore, Professor Waldman’s “gentleness was never tinged by dogmatism, and his instructions were given with an air of frankness and good nature that banished every idea of pedantry" (50). It suggests that Victor’s mentors are revered figures in the scientific community, likely adhered to scientific ethics and respected the boundaries of nature. It is contrary to Victor’s later “unlawful” (56) and “not befitting the human mind” (46) action of creating a life, denoting that Victor’s unethical behavior and eventual deviation from ethical principles are not influenced by his education or mentors but completely his own choices and characteristics. Additionally, the description of how great his mentors are also shows Victor’s hubris and ambitions. By learning from the best, he believes that “among so many men of genius who had directed their inquiries towards the same science, that I alone should be reserved to discover so astonishing a secret.” (52), showing his extreme ambition and overreach that eventually led to his downfall. Lastly, it also shows the classism that only upper-class people have access to such education.

In the chapter, Victor’s self-imposed isolation is reflected in physical isolation, intellectual isolation, and emotional and social isolation. Through his interest in science, Victor "became so ardent and eager that the stars often disappeared in the light of morning whilst [he] was yet engaged in [his] laboratory" (50), “forced to spend days and nights in vaults and charnel-houses” (52), and “the summer months passed while [he] was thus engaged, heart and soul, in one pursuit” (55). It indicates Victor’s physical isolation in that he dedicates all his time and energy to his work, creating a barrier between himself and the outside world. Victor's health declines due to his excessive work habits and lack of self-care. His mental health also suffers as he becomes increasingly obsessed with and detached from reality, and long-term "loathing from [his] occupation" (54) might also distort Victor's human being. Furthermore, Victor isolates himself intellectually. Victor “had become as well acquainted with the theory and practice of natural philosophy as dependent on the lessons of any of the professors at Ingolstadt,” to which he grasps knowledge far more than any students in the college. His research delving into the unknown field of creating life is all conducted alone and not shared with any mentors or peers, leaving him without guidance or ethical consideration. Isolated from ethical guidance from peers and mentors and the moral norms of society, it blinds him to the potential consequences of his actions, leading him down a path of moral ambiguity. Last but not least, the emotional and social isolation is on Victor. Victor spent his undivided attention on his studies and experiments and “could not tear [his] thoughts from [his] employ” (56) so he distances himself from his family and friends and stops communicating regularly with them. It is foreshadowed that Victor will lose and violate his ethics and morality as his father points out that “if the study to which you apply yourself has a tendency to weaken your affections and to destroy your taste for those simple pleasures in which no alloy can possibly mix, then that study is certainty unlawful, that is to say, not befitting the human mind.” (56) The emotional and social isolation leads to his deterioration in relationships, leaving him increasingly alone and misunderstood. This lack of human connection might eventually cause him to lose human beings and minds. Overall, Victor’s isolation foreshadows his further unethical actions in creating life: the potential suffering and rights of the created being, the unforeseen consequences on natural and social orders, and the role of God. It connects to the global issue of ethical science, and the story suggests modern concerns about scientific advancements, such as in genetics and artificial intelligence.
